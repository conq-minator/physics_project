Aim of the Experiment

To systematically determine the energy bandgap (Eg) of semiconductor materials (Silicon and Germanium) by studying the variation of their resistivity with temperature using the Four Probe Method.

Apparatus & Equipment

  • Four Probe Arrangement: Four collinear, equally spaced spring-loaded probes to eliminate contact resistance errors.
  • Semiconductor Samples: High-purity Germanium (Ge) and Silicon (Si) crystal slices.
  • Oven Setup: A controlled heating enclosure capable of reaching 150°C.
  • Constant Current Source: A precision power supply delivering a steady current (in mA).
  • Digital Millivoltmeter: To measure the potential difference across the inner probes.
  • Digital Thermometer: To monitor the absolute temperature of the sample.

Significance

Understanding the bandgap is crucial in semiconductor physics and electrical engineering. It dictates how a material behaves at different temperatures, which is fundamental for designing diodes, transistors, and optoelectronic devices.

Theory and Mathematical Model

1. The Energy Bandgap (Eg)

In solid-state physics, the electrical conductivity of a semiconductor is determined by its band structure. The Valence Band contains electrons strictly bound to the crystal lattice, while the Conduction Band contains free electrons that contribute to electrical current. The void of forbidden energy levels between these two bands is the Energy Bandgap (Eg).

At absolute zero (0 K), an intrinsic semiconductor behaves as a perfect insulator. As the absolute temperature (T) increases, thermal energy excites electrons across the bandgap, creating electron-hole pairs. This drastically increases conductivity and, consequently, decreases the material's electrical resistivity (ρ).

2. Temperature Dependence of Resistivity

The intrinsic carrier concentration follows an exponential Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution. Since resistivity is inversely proportional to carrier concentration, the resistivity of an intrinsic semiconductor at a given temperature is defined by the relation:

ρ = A · exp [ Eg / (2kBT) ]
  • ρ = Resistivity of the semiconductor (Ω·cm)
  • A = A constant depending on the specific geometry and material properties
  • Eg = Bandgap energy of the semiconductor (in electron-volts, eV)
  • kB = Boltzmann constant (8.617 × 10-5 eV/K)
  • T = Absolute temperature (in Kelvin)

3. The Four Probe Principle

Measuring the resistivity of a semiconductor using a standard two-terminal multimeter is highly inaccurate due to contact resistance and minority carrier injection at the boundary between the metal probes and the semiconductor surface. The Four Probe Method eliminates this fatal error.

A constant current (I) is forced through the two outer probes, while a high-impedance voltmeter measures the voltage drop (V) across the two inner probes. Because essentially zero current flows through the voltmeter circuit, contact resistance at the inner probes is completely negated. For a semiconductor slice of thickness W and probe spacing S, the precise resistivity is calculated as:

ρ = (V / I) × 2πS × G(W/S)

4. Deriving the Arrhenius Plot

To extract the bandgap experimentally without dealing with complex exponential curves, we linearize the resistivity equation by taking the base-10 logarithm on both sides:

log10(ρ) = [ Eg / (2.303 × 2kB) ] × (1 / T) + log10(A)

By plotting log10(ρ) on the Y-axis against 103/T on the X-axis, the resulting data forms a straight line (an Arrhenius Plot) with a distinct slope (m). We can then rearrange the slope formula to solve for the exact experimental Bandgap energy:

Eg = m × 4.606 × kB × 103 (in eV)

Experimental Procedure

Phase 1: Initialization

  1. Navigate to the Simulation tab on the left dashboard.
  2. In the Control Deck, select your target Semiconductor Material (Germanium or Silicon). Notice the sample visual update accordingly.
  3. Click the System Power button to activate the constant current source and the digital displays.
  4. Adjust the Constant Current (I) slider to a standard testing value (e.g., 5.0 mA). Leave this slider completely untouched for the remainder of the trial.

Phase 2: Data Collection

  1. Observe the initial Voltage (mV) reading at room temperature (25°C).
  2. Click the purple Record Reading button. The system will automatically calculate the resistivity (ρ), 10³/T, and log&sub1;&sub0;(ρ), logging it into the Observation Table.
  3. Slowly increase the Oven Temperature (T) slider in increments of roughly 10°C to 15°C.
  4. Watch the simulated oven glow increase and the voltage drop as thermal energy excites more electrons into the conduction band.
  5. At each new temperature interval, click Record Reading. Ensure you collect at least 5 distinct data points to establish a reliable trend line.

Phase 3: Graphical Analysis & Calculation

  1. Observe the Arrhenius Plot dynamically generating your data points, plotting log&sub1;&sub0;(ρ) on the Y-axis against 10³/T on the X-axis.
  2. Once 5 points are recorded, the Calculate Bandgap button will unlock.
  3. Click it to command the simulation engine to perform a linear regression. The system will draw the line of best fit, calculate its slope, and output the final experimental Bandgap (Eg) in eV.
  4. Compare your experimental result with the standard theoretical values (Ge ≈ 0.66 eV, Si ≈ 1.12 eV).
